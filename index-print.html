§ 01 About Bio · Practice
On infrastructure, territory, and the long argument the mountain has always had with the road.

Prince Tomar is a doctoral researcher at the Centre for Landscape and Culture, Tallinn University. He works with borderland communities along the Indian Himalaya, with a particular focus on the dynamics of road development — tracing the subtle interplay between infrastructure, territorialisation, and the region's intricate dance of history, geopolitics, and terrain.

"The road is not only the thing that arrives; it is also the question the village must learn to answer."

Beyond the academic work, he writes poems and is interested in the porous border between ethnography and literary studies — the way a field note can become a stanza, and a stanza can carry the weight of a census. His current project studies the spatial-temporal dimensions of change in the Indian Himalaya as the pace of road development quickens.

He keeps an open notebook here: photographs from the field, dispatches and small experiments with AI, and an epilogue of things being read, watched, listened to.

§ 05 Selected Writing Poem

And the road, like a long sentence, arrives.

Ridge, with bulldozer
First the dust learned a new direction. Then the goats. Then the names of the goats. By winter the mountain had agreed to be measured in kilometres.
A road, I think, is a kind of grammar — it puts a verb where there used to be only the long, slow noun of a wall.
— Excerpt · Field journal, Nubra, 2024
